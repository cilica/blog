80 - 20 rule

Mircea's stuff
2019-12-03

The 80-20 rule, otherwise called the Pareto principle, states that, for many events, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes.

This principle can be observed all over our lives:

  • 80% of sales come from 20% of clients
  • 20% of patients use 80% of health care resources.
  • 20% of the world’s population controlling 80% of the world’s income
  • 20% of the exercises and habits have 80% of the impact in sports traingins, etc.

In programming, this principle can be observed as well:

  • Microsoft noted that by fixing the top 20% of the most-reported bugs, 80% of the related errors and crashes in a given system would be eliminated
  • 20 percent of the code has 80 percent of the errors
  • 80% of a certain piece of software can be written in 20% of the total allocated time

Take for example this tweet/meme/joke:

Usually programmers on a fresh project get a lot of things done very quickly: the structure and all the major functional elements and this make them, the management, clients (in a word: stakeholders), think that the whole project will be done in several months or so. However, usually it is severily underestimated the remaining 20% of work, which - you got it - takes up 80% of time: requirements management, documentation, tests, automated builds, reviews, scripts, intricate interactions, functional safety anlysis, static code analysis, quality goals, fixing that elusive sporadic bug, etc.

In better words:

The first 90 percent of the code accounts for the first 90 percent of the development time. The remaining 10 percent of the code accounts for the other 90 percent of the development time.
— Tom Cargill, Bell Labs

If you have that rule in mind, a project made in 180% of estimated time, is actually a good project, from experience point of view. That’s why it’s not uncommon for people in software engineering to double their initial estimates for a, somewhat, accurate estimate.
